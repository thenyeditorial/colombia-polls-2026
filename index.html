The Tiger, the Senator, and the Mathematician: Colombia's Three-Way War for the Presidency

With 107 pre-candidates whittled to a handful, an assassinated senator, a Bukele-style outsider surging on the right, and Petro's heir leading on the left, Colombia's May 31 election is the most volatile and consequential race in Latin America this year. We analyze every available poll and four decades of electoral history.

Colombia is heading into the most unpredictable election in its modern democratic history. On May 31, over 40 million voters will choose their next president in a race where the two frontrunners — a far-right lawyer nicknamed "El Tigre" and a left-wing senator who spent decades fighting ex-President Álvaro Uribe in court — are now locked in a statistical tie. Between them stands a fractured center, a congressional election on March 8 that will reshape the landscape, and a country convulsed by record coca production, an assassination that shook the political class, a 23% minimum-wage hike, and a U.S. administration that has decertified Colombia as a drug-war ally. No candidate is expected to win 50% in the first round. A runoff on June 21 is nearly certain. And in that runoff, the mathematics of coalition-building — who inherits the center's voters — will determine whether Colombia continues its first-ever leftist government or swings hard to the right.

The trajectory is unmistakable. In November, Cepeda led by 14 points. By February, the race is a dead heat. De la Espriella's rise mirrors the pattern of right-wing outsiders across Latin America — Bukele in El Salvador, Milei in Argentina — who consolidate anti-establishment sentiment through social media, hardline security rhetoric, and a deliberate rejection of traditional party structures. The "Defender of the Homeland" kits he ships to supporters are a marketing stroke borrowed directly from the Milei playbook.

But the numbers hide a critical asymmetry. Cepeda's support has a floor — the ~24% of Colombians who identify as left-leaning plus Petro's still-solid 38% approval base. De la Espriella's support has a ceiling problem in a runoff: he is the most polarizing candidate in the field, and a Centro Nacional de Consultoría poll found that in a runoff against Fajardo, De la Espriella wins by only 37.9% to 23.2% — meaning nearly 39% of voters reject both. The question is whether antipathy toward the left is strong enough to push centrist voters into the arms of a far-right populist, or whether they abstain.

The 2022 results contain three lessons for 2026. First, the left has a proven ceiling of ~40% in the first round and ~50% in a runoff — Petro couldn't exceed it even in a wave year. Cepeda, who lacks Petro's personal charisma and carries the burden of Petro's scandals, will likely perform below this ceiling. Second, the "outsider" lane — Hernández's 28% came from nowhere, fueled by anti-corruption TikTok campaigning — is exactly the lane De la Espriella is now occupying, but with far more organizational sophistication. Third, the center collapses under polarization. Fajardo went from 4 million votes in 2018 to 888,000 in 2022 — an 80% decline. If 2026 is another polarized binary, Fajardo will be squeezed again.

The critical arithmetic: in the 2022 runoff, Petro gained ~2.75 million votes from the first round. Hernández gained ~4.6 million — absorbing almost the entire Gutiérrez + Fajardo vote. The right-of-center consolidation in a runoff is more efficient than the left's. This is why the CNC runoff simulation shows De la Espriella beating Cepeda by 9.3 points: anti-Petro sentiment is a more powerful unifier than pro-Petro enthusiasm.

"Fifty percent of respondents said they would prefer a candidate opposed to Petro. The math is clear: the anti-Petro vote is larger than the pro-Petro vote. The question is whether it consolidates behind one person."
— Invamer poll analysis, November 2025
04

Seven Variables That Will Shape May 31

1. March 8: The Bellwether. Congressional elections and three presidential primaries will reveal real voter mobilization. The right-wing "Gran Consulta" will choose between Paloma Valencia, Juan Manuel Galán, Vicky Dávila, and others. If Valencia wins big, it consolidates the traditional Uribista base behind a candidate who could either challenge or ally with De la Espriella. If the consultation draws low turnout, it signals that the right's energy is already with El Tigre.

2. Uribe's Senate gambit. Former President Álvaro Uribe — the most consequential Colombian politician of the 21st century — placed himself at position 25 on the Centro Democrático Senate list. His party would need to win at least 25 seats for him to return. This is mathematically difficult but symbolically enormous. If Uribe returns to the Senate, it energizes the right and could create a kingmaker dynamic. If he fails, it signals the twilight of Uribismo as an organizational force.

3. Security and political violence. The 2025 assassination of Senator Miguel Uribe Turbay — shot at a rally, dying weeks later — was a seismic event. Security is now the top voter concern alongside corruption. Record coca production (262,000 hectares in 2024), armed group violence in Arauca and Meta, and Petro's struggling "Total Peace" program have created a "seguridad democrática" nostalgia that benefits hardliners like De la Espriella, who promises fumigation and "democratic authority."

4. The U.S. factor. The Trump administration decertified Colombia as a drug-war partner, sanctioned Petro's family for alleged organized-crime links, and engaged in public confrontations. An Invamer poll found 78–81% of Colombians say their next president must have good relations with Washington. This massively disadvantages Cepeda, who is seen as Petro's heir and carries the association with anti-American rhetoric. De la Espriella, who openly admires Trump, benefits.

5. The economy. Colombia suspended its fiscal rule through 2027. The 23% minimum-wage hike in December 2025 raises inflation fears. GDP growth is below the 21st-century average. Nearly 58% of the workforce is informal. Morgan Stanley analysts warn the next administration will "inherit a difficult fiscal situation." Bread-and-butter concerns could override ideological loyalty.

6. Petro's approval — floor or ceiling? At 35.7% (AtlasIntel, January 2026), Petro's approval is stable but not strong. Analyst Sergio Guzmán argues this isn't about policy but identity: "This is the first government that looks like the country" — with Afro-Colombians and marginalized communities in senior positions. That symbolic power gives the left a durable base, but the 53.5% disapproval is the gravitational force pushing the race rightward.

7. The center's existential dilemma. Fajardo, Claudia López, Juan Manuel Galán, and others collectively poll at 15–20% — enough to be kingmakers in a runoff but not enough to make the runoff. If the center fragments (likely), its voters become the prize in the second round. In 2022, center voters broke overwhelmingly for the right in the runoff. If this pattern repeats, De la Espriella wins. If Cepeda can build a "broad front" — as he has called for — the math gets competitive.

06

The Runoff Math: Why the Right Is Favored

The structural arithmetic of a Colombian runoff currently favors the right, for three interlocking reasons.

First, the anti-Petro majority. Petro's disapproval is 53.5%. A November Invamer poll found 50% of voters want a candidate opposed to Petro. Cepeda is running explicitly as Petro's heir. In a binary runoff, the "anyone but Petro's party" vote is larger than the "continue the project" vote.

Second, center-voter behavior in 2022. When Fajardo's 888,000 first-round voters and Gutiérrez's 5 million had to choose between Petro and Hernández, the overwhelming majority went right. Hernández gained 4.6 million votes between rounds while Petro gained 2.75 million. If this pattern repeats — center breaks right — De la Espriella's runoff advantage is decisive.

Third, the rejection ceiling. Cepeda's "would never vote for" rate is 23.9% — among the highest in the field. De la Espriella is polarizing too, but in a two-person race, the candidate with the lower rejection ceiling typically wins because they have more room to grow.

However, the left has counterarguments. Petro's floor of ~38% is larger than any single right-wing candidate's base. If Cepeda can hold that floor and peel off 12–13% from the center — positioning himself as the democratic moderate against far-right populism — the math works. The fear of a Bukele-style strongman could push moderate voters left rather than right, especially urban professionals and women. This is Cepeda's strategic bet.

The Key Number: 2.82 Million That's how many Venezuelan migrants and refugees Colombia hosts — the largest such population in Latin America. Immigration policy, border security, and the economic strain of integration are live campaign issues. De la Espriella has made hardline immigration rhetoric a centerpiece. This mirrors the nativist turn across Latin America and the U.S. and could mobilize voters who feel economically displaced.
07

March 8: The Election Before the Election

Congressional elections and three primaries will reshape the presidential race

Before May 31, Colombia votes on March 8 for its entire Congress — 108 Senators and 188 House members — and simultaneously holds three presidential coalition primaries. This is the real starting gun.

What to watch: the right-wing "Gran Consulta por Colombia" primary (18.7% of voters say they'll participate) will choose among Paloma Valencia, Juan Carlos Pinzón, Vicky Dávila, Aníbal Gaviria, and Juan Daniel Oviedo. Valencia leads among likely participants at 19.1%. A strong showing by her could either consolidate the right behind Centro Democrático or, if she underperforms, leave De la Espriella as the unchallenged right-wing standard-bearer.

The leftist "Pacto Amplio" primary (29.8% plan to participate) features Roy Barreras as the leading contender. Since Cepeda was barred from participating, this consultation's winner becomes either his ally or his rival on the left — a critical dynamic.

Congressional results will also reveal whether Uribe's gambit succeeds, whether the Pacto Histórico can hold its caucus, and how the proliferation of coalition lists (six in the Senate alone, up from one in 2018) fragments the landscape. The political machines — the maquinarias — are strongest in congressional races, where clientelistic networks and vote-buying are endemic. These machines will have less influence in the presidential race, where the voto de opinión (independent, ideological vote) is more dominant.

08

The Bottom Line

Our assessment: Colombia's 2026 presidential election is a genuine three-way race that will almost certainly produce a runoff. In that runoff, the structural advantages — anti-Petro majority, center-voter behavior, rejection ceilings — currently favor the right. We assign a 35% probability to a De la Espriella presidency, 25% to Cepeda, 20% to a centrist making the runoff and reshuffling the math, and 20% to consolidation or wildcard scenarios.

But Colombia defies forecasts. In 2022, Rodolfo Hernández rose from 3% to the runoff in six weeks. In 2018, Petro went from perennial also-ran to 8.5 million first-round votes. In 2002, Álvaro Uribe surged from third place to a first-round victory. Colombian presidential races are defined by late movement, and the March 8 primaries will reshape the field in ways no current model can fully anticipate.

What we can say with confidence: the next president of Colombia will govern a country with record cocaine production, 2.82 million Venezuelan migrants, a suspended fiscal rule, armed group violence in its countryside, a fractured relationship with Washington, and a population where 37% lean right, 24% lean left, and the rest are up for grabs. Whoever inherits the Casa de Nariño on August 7 will need not just a mandate but a miracle.

"The left must build coalitions beyond ideology. The center must consolidate behind one name. The right cannot campaign solely on anti-Petro sentiment — it needs a compelling agenda. And everyone is running out of time."
— Sergio Guzmán, Colombia Risk Analysis, October 2025
